**A capacitor is a device that separates positive and negative charges onto two metal plates divided with a thin insulating gap.**
Often in a cylinder shape to maximise surface area

![[Capacitor.png|267]]

A capacitor forms a potential difference when charged

$C = \frac{\epsilon_{0}A}{d}$ (f.b.)

$\varepsilon_{0} = \text{ permitivity of free space}$ (f.b.)

The two plates of a capacitor always have equal and opposite charge

Capacitor is fully charged when $V = \epsilon$ and the net charge is 0

The capacitance, C, of a capacitor is defined as the charge Q stored per unit p.d. across it.

$C = \frac{Q}{V}$ (f.b.)


Capacitors in parallel sum their capacitance

$C_{T} = C_{1} + C_{2} \dots$ (f.b.)


Capacitors in series sum the reciprocals to get the reciprocal

$\frac{1}{C_{T}} = \frac{1}{C_{1}} + \frac{1}{C_{2}}\dots$ (f.b.)

## Energy in capacitors

The energy stored in a charged capacitor is equal to the total area under the V-Q graph
as $W=VQ$

$W = \frac{1}{2}QV$ (f.b.)

The gradient of a V-Q graph gives you the reciprocal of the capacitance

Half of the energy charging a capacitor is lost (hence the $\frac{1}{2}$) doing work against the electrostatic repulsion of the like charges

## Discharging

When a capacitor discharges through a fixed resistor, the charge, current and potential difference all decrease *exponentially* with time, as all three quantities are proportional to each other

### $x = x_{0} e^\frac{-t}{cr}$ 
(f.b.)

You can replace $x$ with: $Q, I \text{ and } V$ to get this family of equations:
- $Q = Q_{0}e^{ \frac{-t}{CR}}$
- $I = I_{0}e^{ \frac{-t}{CR}}$
- $V = V_{0}e^{ \frac{-t}{CR}}$

The formula book only gives the top one with $x$, the general one


**The ratio between values separated by the same time interval will be constant**


**The time constant of a capacitor $\tau$ is the time taken for the charge (or current or p.d.) to decrease to $e^{-1}$ of its original value:**

$\tau = CR$ (f.b.)

## Charging

**When charging a capacitor, the current in the circuit decreases exponentially as the capacitor plates become occupied by charge**

As you charge a capacitor, the other components in the circuit decrease in their p.d. and charge according to the above equations, which means that (with some re-arranging that I really can't be bothered to put here) you can get the below formula

$x = x_{0}(1-e^{\frac{-t}{CR}} )$ (f.b.)

You can replace $x$ with $V_{c}$ or $Q$ to get:
- $V_{c} = V_{0}(1-e^{\frac{-t}{CR}} )$ 
- $Q = Q_{0}(1-e^{\frac{-t}{CR}} )$
