Electric current is the flow of charge through wires and components. Electrons flow from the negative terminal to the positive one*

The size of electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge Q

$Q = I \times t$ (f.b.)
Charge, Q, in Coulombs, C
Current, I, in Amps, A
Time, t, in Seconds, s


Millikan's oil drop experiment found that  charge was always a whole number multiple of the fundamental charge, e, ($1.6\times {10}^{-19} C$) (f.b.)

\* Real current, but for nearly everything you use conventional current because physicists don't know how to let go and admit they were wrong once

## Kirchoff's 1'st law

also see Kirchoff's 2nd law

Kirchoff's 1st law: For any point in an electrical circuit, the sum of currents into that point is the sum of currents out of that point

written fancy: $\Sigma I_{in} = \Sigma I_{out}$

add up all the currents going into an intersection and that has to equal adding up all the currents going out of the intersection

## Mean drift Velocity


Number density is the number of "things" per unit volume of anything

Metals have one free electron per atom, meaning that the average number density of charge carriers in metals is ~$10^{28}m^{-3}$

You can group materials into three different groups depending on their number density of charge carriers: Insulators, Semi-conductors and Conductors

![[number density of charge carriers.png]]

more charge carriers per unit volume, the better the thing is at carrying current


Electrons average velocity is very slow (< 1mm $s^{-1}$), but they respond so rapidly because all of the free electrons are already in the loop and start moving at once, similar to like a chain loop

The mean drift velocity is the mean displacement the free electrons travel per unit time between collisions towards the positive terminal

$I = Aven$ (f.b.)
A = cross sectional area
v = mean electron drift velocity
e = elementary charge
n = number density of charge carriers
