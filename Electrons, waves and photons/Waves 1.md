## Progressive waves
**A progressive wave is an oscillation that travels through matter or a vacuum, transferring energy without transferring matter**

**Transverse waves have oscillations perpendicular to the direction of the wave motion**
Think like a sin or cos wave
They have high points (peaks) and low points (troughs)

Examples include: EM waves, S type earthquakes (S looks like a sin wave on it's side)

**Longitudinal waves have oscillations parallel to the direction of the wave motion**

such as this slinky:
![[Slinky longitudinal.png]]


They have dense / compressed points (compressions) and low density points (rarefactions)

Examples include: Sound waves, P type earthquakes

Longitudinal waves can't travel through a vacuum while transverse waves can

## Wave properties

**Displacement, s: the distance & direction from its equilibrium position of a vibrating particle (m)**

**Amplitude, A: the maximum displacement**

**Wavelength $\lambda$, : the minimum distance between two points in phase on a wave e.g. from one peak to the next**


![[Wave properties diagram.png]]

**The period of oscillation, T is the time taken for one oscillation (s)**

**A cycle of a wave is one complete full oscillation**

**The frequency f is the number of cycles per second (Hz)**

$f=\frac{1}{T}$ (f.b.)

A wave travels a distance of one wavelength in the time period of 1 cycle, so:

$V=\frac{\lambda}{T},\text{ as }f=\frac{1}{T}, \therefore v=f\lambda$ (f.b.)

**The phase difference $\phi$ is the difference in displacements of particles along a wave, or between displacements of particles on different waves, measured in degrees or radians**

$360 \text{ degrees} = 2\pi \text{ radians}$


If two waves are perfectly in step with each other, we say they are in phase and $\phi = 0$

if two points on a wave are distance x away from each other, then:
$\phi = \frac{x}{\lambda}\times{360} \text{ or } \frac{x}{\lambda} \times 2\pi$

## Reflection & Refraction


**Wavefronts are theoretical lines on a wave that represent the same phase (e.g. crest), drawn perpendicular to the direction of travel and separated by the wavelength**


![[Wavefronts.png|490]]

**Law of reflection: The angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection, with no change in wavelength, frequency or wave velocity**

![[Incidience = reflection.png|470]]

These angles are always drawn to the normal of the surface


**Refraction occurs when a wave changes direction as it changes speed passing from one medium to another. e.g. light slows down in glass**

The frequency remains constant

The wavefronts remain continuous across the boundary and so the wave changes direction.

**Light slows down in denser materials and so bends towards the normal**

and the opposite happens going from more dense to less dense

There is also always some partial reflections

## Intensity

**The intensity of a progressive wave is the power passing through a surface per unit area**

$I=\frac{P}{A}$ (f.b.)

P is the radiant power passing through a surface
I is the intensity of the wave at the surface
A is the cross sectional area

#### Inverse square law

If a point emits light in all directions, then as the wave spreads out, the intensity reduces

$I=\frac{P}{A} = \frac{P}{4\pi r^{2}} \therefore I \propto \frac{1}{r^{2}}$


**The intensity is proportional to the wave amplitude squared**

$\text{ intensity} \propto \text{(amplitude)}^{2}$

## Snell's law of refraction

**The refractive index of a material n is defined as the ratio of the speed of light in a vacuum c and the speed in the material v**

$n=\frac{c}{v}$(f.b.) (unitless)

![[refractiver index list.png|250]]

You should know roughly the order of these, importantly that air is basically the same as a vacuum

**Snell's law states that when light passes from one material to another the product of the refractive index and the sine of the angle to the normal is constant**

$n\sin\theta = \text{constant}$ (f.b.)

or

$n_{1}\sin \theta_{1} = n_{2}\sin\theta_{2}$

remember its always to the normal

entering higher refractive index = bend towards the normal

**Total internal reflection is where all the light is reflected at a boundary when travelling from a medium with a lower refractive index to one with a higher one**
**This only occurs when the angle of incidence is above the critical angle C for the two mediums**


$\sin C = \frac{1}{n}$(f.b.) for passing from air to material
