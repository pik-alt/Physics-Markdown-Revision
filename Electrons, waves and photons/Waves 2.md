## Superposition

**When two waves of the same type meet they superpose to produce a single wave where the displacement at each point is equal to the sum of displacements of the individual waves**

![[wave superpositio.png|432]]


If two waves are in phase, then they interfere only constructively
If two waves are in anti-phase, then they only interfere destructively


This is how noise cancelling headphones work, they play the inverse frequency to what's playing and the two waves destructively interfere



## Interference


**Two waves are coherent if they have a constant phase difference, and therefore the same frequency**


The interference pattern from two coherent waves superposing create maxima at points of constructive interference and minima at points of destructive interference

![[Interference pattern.png|474]]


Wherever the wavefronts overlap, that's a maxima and in between them are the minima

**The path difference is the difference in distance that two waves travel**

![[Path difference.png]]


$A = B, \text{ path difference = 0}, \therefore \phi = 0 \therefore \text{ central maxima}$

![[minima path differenece.png]]


$C-D = \frac{1}{2}\lambda,\text{ path difference = } \frac{1}{2}\lambda \therefore \phi = 180 \text{ or } \pi \therefore \text{ first order minima}$


**If the path difference is a whole number multiple of a wavelength, that creates a maxima at that point
If it's a whole multiple and a half wavelengths, that creates a minima**

**$\phi = \text{ even} \times \pi, \text{ maxima}$
$\phi = \text{ odd} \times \pi, \text{ minima}$**

You can test this with sound waves, having two speakers play the same frequency and, at different points, the sound will be very loud or almost mute

## Stationary waves

**A stationary wave is a wave that remains in a constant position with no net energy transfer**

This happens when two waves interfere with each other in the right way, or by one wave being generated and reflected back onto itself

[Stationary waves animation](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ic73oZoqr70)
Here's a demonstration

The two waves must have the same frequency.

**Nodes: Fixed points where the amplitude is always zero**
**Antinodes: Fixed points where the amplitude is always at a maximum**

![[Nodes antinodes.png]]

The distance between two nodes is always $\frac{\lambda}{2}$

### Tricky thing they always like to try and get you with!!
**Between adjacent nodes all particles are oscillating in phase with each other $\phi = 0$**

![[standing phase difference.png|537]]

Despite them being on different parts of the wave, if they're within two nodes, any two particles are in phase with each other

**On different sides of a node all particles are in anti-phase, $\phi = \pi$**

## Harmonics

A plucked string (such as on a guitar or a violin) creates a pulse which reflects at a fixed end. The reflected wave travels in opposite directions to the initial plucked wave and so they interfere, producing stationary waves.

The speed of a stationary wave depends on the string tension and the mass per meter:

$v=\sqrt{ \frac{T}{\mu} }$
$T$ = tension
$\mu$ = mass per meter

(not expected to know the formula)

**The ends of a string forming a standing wave are always nodes**

![[String harmonics.png]]


1 Anti-node = 1st fundamental frequency
2 Anti-nodes = 2nd fundamental frequency
and so on...

n anti nodes means that it is the nth fundamental frequency
and $\lambda_{n} = \frac{n\lambda_{1}}{2}$
