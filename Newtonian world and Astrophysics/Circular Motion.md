
Circular motion describes any motion around a complete circular path

In A-level, we only deal with motion where the angular velocity is constant

**Angular displacement $\theta$ is the angle in radians through which a point revolves around a centre**

you need to be fluent in converting between radians and degrees using:
$360^{\circ} = 2\pi$ 

for a full rotation, $\theta = 2\pi$
if this rotation takes $T$ seconds, then after $t$ seconds,
$\theta = 2\pi \times\frac{t}{T}$

Frequency $f$ is $\frac{1}{T}$, therefore $\theta = 2\pi tf$ 

---
## Angular Velocity

**Angular velocity $\omega$ is the rate of change of angle, in rad $s^{-1}$ **

$\omega = \frac{\Delta\theta}{\Delta t}$

when $\theta = 2\pi, t = T$
$\therefore$
$\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T}$ or $\omega = 2\pi f$


### Linear Velocity

This is the straight line velocity, tangent to the circular path

$v = \frac{s}{t} = \frac{2\pi r(circumference)}{T}$


as $\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T}, v=\omega r$

---

## Centripetal Acceleration

Since velocity is a vector and is constantly changing direction, that means that there's a change in velocity, meaning that there's an acceleration.
This acceleration acts towards the centre.

$a=\frac{V^{2}}{r}$
substituting $v=\omega r$ you get $a=\omega^{2}r$


## Centripetal Force

Newton's 1st law states that if there's an acceleration, there's a resultant force that causes the acceleration

**Centripetal Force is the resultant force that acts on an object in circular motion that always acts towards the centre**

It's not an additional force added, it's just whatever force is happening to act towards the centre

$F=\frac{mv^{2}}{r} \text{ or } F=m\omega^{2}r$

#### More on Centripetal Force

![[swinging bung.png]]

This is a very common example / question asked

The weight of the bung is what's providing the centripetal force for the mass

$Mg = m\omega^{2}r$
$\therefore$
$r=\frac{Mg}{m\omega^{2}}$

so if you increase the angular speed ($\omega$), for the same mass bung, the radius has to decrease

#### Loop the Loop

![[Loo-the-loop.png]]


The motorcyclist doesn't fall down when at the top of the loop if the centripetal acceleration towards the centre is greater than or equal to their weight pulling them down

$a \geq g$

$\frac{v^{2}}{r} = g$

$v_{min} = \sqrt{ gr }$

#### car over curve
![[car over curve.png]]

The weight of the car provides the centripetal force, however it is always being counteracted by the normal reaction force

$mg-N = \frac{mv^{2}}{r}$

$N= mg-\frac{mv^{2}}{r}$

At the point when the car leaves the ground, there is no reaction force, meaning $N=0$

$mg=\frac{mv^{2}}{r}$, $g=\frac{v^{2}}{r}$ , $v=\sqrt{ gr }$   where v is the minimum speed needed to leave the road
